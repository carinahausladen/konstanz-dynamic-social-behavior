Opening threads
Three questions to bring with you
1
Is this reasoning, or is it pattern-matching? When the model cooperates or defects, is it reasoning about the game or reproducing patterns from training text that describes such games?
2
Does any of it generalise beyond toy games? Every empirical paper here uses stylised, short-horizon, binary, stakes-free interactions. The lab-to-field gap that has haunted behavioural econ applies here too.
3
What is the field not asking? The canonical experiments have been run. A new model on a new 2×2 is incremental. Where is the genuine frontier?
Hold the first two for the papers. The third is what Part II is about.
🟦 Horton · The programmatic move
LLMs as theory tool, not substitute

Prompt with a persona. Ask a scenario question. Watch responses shift in systematic ways when the persona shifts.

Socialist vs. libertarian

Same fairness scenario → different answers.

Equity vs. efficiency

Same dictator game → different allocations.

Min-wage variation

Same labour-market persona → hiring shifts.

Homo silicus, in parallel to Homo economicus. Stipulated, not empirical. A modelling convention.
Modest claim: LLMs as cheap hypothesis pre-test, not equivalence to human subjects.
🟦 Horton · What to carry forward
Three design choices that will echo

1. Agent by prompt

Heterogeneity is stipulated by the experimenter, not recovered from data.

2. Directional criterion

Pools across runs. Asks only whether the aggregate moves the way humans did.

3. Singular silicon

One agent per persona, characterised by central tendency. One GPT-4 or many? Not asked.

Five paradigms re-run: fairness (Kahneman), dictator (Charness–Rabin), status quo (Samuelson–Zeckhauser), prospect (Oprea), hiring.
Old question reopened with a new instrument: what kind of object is Homo economicus?
🟩 Sun et al. · Four-branch taxonomy
The map of the field

§2 — LLMs in game playgrounds

Run LLMs through games, measure play. Where almost all our W2+W3 papers live.

§3 — GT methods to improve LLMs

Shapley, social choice, max-min. GT as tool for the model.

§4 — Games about LLMs

Developer competition, deployer incentives, societal externalities.

§5 — LLMs to advance GT

Intractable games, natural-language moves. LLM as method, not subject.

Horton Lorè Fan MindGames Akata Willis → all §2. Kozlowski & Evans → straddles §2/§3.4. Centaur → outside the taxonomy entirely.
🟩 Sun et al. · What's missing
§3.3 "heterogeneity" does not mean what you think

What §3.3 is

Aligning LLMs to heterogeneous users.

Heterogeneity in the audience.

What it is not

The model's own behavioural variability.

One GPT-4 or many? Not in the taxonomy.

No within-model types. No mixture models. No latent-class analysis. No DTW clustering. No IRL on trajectories. Thirty years of behavioural-GT machinery — absent from the map.
Patterned heterogeneity of LLM behaviour is a category the taxonomy does not contain. Part II returns to this.
🟩 Sun §2 · "Benefit humanity" · 1 of 4
Knowing where LLMs break strategically — before deployment

§2 runs LLMs through games and catalogues the failures. The payoff is a shortlist of documented deployment hazards, backed by experimental evidence — so policy and deployment conversations can reference evidence rather than intuition.

Antitrust

LLMs display tacit collusion in pricing in Bertrand-competition games (Sun Table 1).

Consequence: any platform deploying LLMs as pricing agents.

Negotiation

Models "regress to selfish or inconsistent strategies in complex scenarios without social scaffolding."

Consequence: LLMs representing coalitions or interests.

Role reliability

Loss of role consistency and logical coherence under pressure (Avalon, Werewolf).

Consequence: citizen service, legal aid, medical triage.

Evidence-backed hazard list — where not to trust LLMs, empirically, not by intuition.
🟩 Sun §3 · "Benefit humanity" · 2 of 4
Accountability, fairness, stability inside the model

§3 uses game-theoretic tools to patch four documented failure modes of LLM pipelines. The downstream consequences are directly political.

Audit-readiness

Shapley values decompose decisions into the contributions of inputs, layers, attention heads. Makes models auditable.

Pre-condition for GDPR's "right to explanation" and the EU AI Act.

Honest alignment

Min-max recasting of RLHF drops the Bradley–Terry assumption that preferences are transitive and scalar — real preferences, including voting preferences, are neither.

Alignment that does not misrepresent the people it serves.

Fair representation

Sun reports canonical RLHF is mathematically equivalent to Borda count — a voting rule with tyranny-of-the-majority properties. Social-choice axioms and mechanism design fix this.

Models that do not silently under-serve minority subgroups.

Stable over time

Self-play and Stackelberg training prevent reward-hacking as the model improves past its static reward signal.

Less drift, less brittleness.

🟩 Sun §4 · "Benefit humanity" · 3 of 4
Predicting the AI rollout market before bad equilibria lock in

§4 treats LLMs as institutional players and models the strategic equilibria their deployment produces — leverage for regulators and platform designers.

Braess's Paradox for GenAI

Taitler et al.: a platform optimising for revenue drains the human knowledge forum it depends on (Stack Overflow-style). Everyone ends up worse off.

Named system-level failure a regulator can aim policy at.

Regulatory arbitrage

Laufer et al.: safety standards only on downstream fine-tuners incentivise upstream developers to underinvest in safety.

Textbook econ-of-regulation pathology, applied to AI.

Data-corruption incentives

Buening et al. derive payment rules that stop RLHF annotators from strategically misreporting preferences.

Stops training data being corrupted by annotators' own incentives.

This is the branch where "governance of AI" becomes a concrete research object.
🟩 Sun §5 · "Benefit humanity" · 4 of 4
Mechanism design for language-based governance

Classical mechanism design requires problems expressible in numbers — which excludes most coordination problems social scientists care about. LLMs remove that restriction.

Generative social choice

Fish et al., Boehmer et al.: extract preferences from free-text deliberation, produce representative policy slates.

Application: Polis-style citizens' assemblies.

Semantic-enhanced auctions

Sun et al.: run Vickrey and combinatorial auctions on natural-language valuations.

Application: public procurement with qualitative criteria.

Verbalised Bayesian persuasion

Li et al.: models dialogue in real diplomatic and multi-stakeholder negotiation.

Application: mediation, treaty bargaining.

Mechanism-as-code

Liu et al.: LLM writes the mechanism itself as human-readable pseudocode; rediscovers classical ones in competitive evaluation.

Application: policy rule-writing with machine auditability.

🟪 Binz et al. · Centaur — the fifth move
Fine-tuning, not prompting — the paper whose title most directly matches this course

Every other paper on this reading list prompts an existing LLM. Centaur fine-tunes one — Llama 3.1 70B adapted to Psych-101, a corpus of 160 psychology experiments transcribed into natural language (60,092 participants, 10.7M choices). The training objective predicts the subject's next choice, not the next word of the stimulus.

The methodological move

Training, not prompting. The distinction is the one you know from econometrics between using someone else's published model and re-estimating its parameters on your own data.

What it predicts

Trial-level human choices on 32 held-out psychology experiments, beating both base Llama and the best domain-specific cognitive model. Generalises to modified cover stories, new problem structures, LSAT items.

Why it is 🟪

Sits outside Sun's four-branch taxonomy entirely — not evaluation, not alignment fix, not game-about-LLM, not LLM-for-GT. A fifth methodological move.

The paper on this reading list whose title most directly matches Computational Methods for Modeling Dynamic Social Behavior. → Part II picks it up again at sl26 as the one partial counterexample to the missed-mixture diagnosis.
🟨 Static behavioural evaluation
What the yellow category does

The simplest move in the literature:

Take a catalogue of games. Run an LLM through them many times. Report how often it plays each strategy.

No iteration. No opponent learning. No context beyond the prompt. Unit of analysis: the model. Datum: the proportion choosing each action. Comparison: across models, games, framings.

Lorè & Heydari

Tight factorial.

Fan et al.

Broader battery.

MindGames

Packaged as a benchmark.

This is the aggregate-means playbook of early behavioural GT, applied to a new subject.
🟨 Lorè & Heydari · Design & three axes
3 × 4 × 5 factorial — what payoffs and what cover story?

3 models

GPT-3.5 · GPT-4 · Llama-2 70B

4 games

PD · Stag Hunt · Snowdrift · Harmony

5 contexts

Summit · business · environmental · friends · teammates

axis GPT-3.5 GPT-4 Llama-2 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── response to payoffs weak strong strong response to framing strong modulated calibrated within-cell distribution weakly mixed near-bimodal, pure genuinely mixed
Reasoner signature: high structure, low framing. Pattern-matcher: the reverse. GPT-4 sits in between.
🟨 Lorè & Heydari · The key signal
The GPT-4 bimodality
300 identical prompts. Distribution bunches at the two extremes of the response scale.
In human data, bimodality across runs of the same manipulation is a textbook signature of a two-type population. It is what drove the 1990s decomposition of PGG contributors into conditional cooperators and free-riders.

Lorè and Heydari report the bimodality. They do not fit it as a mixture.

Hold this in mind. Part II returns to it.
🟨 Fan et al. · MindGames · Wider nets, same silence
Scaling the static-evaluation move

Fan et al. 2024

Wider battery, explicit rationality criterion (dominance, backward induction, best-response).

Reports rationality scores per model per game.

Same unit of analysis: hundreds of runs per cell, within-cell distribution not examined.

MindGames 2025

Packages theory-of-mind + strategy items as a benchmark.

Operationalises Akata's BoS ToM failure across many models.

Benchmark framing collapses within-model variability before it can be seen.

A 0.78 rationality score could be one coherent reasoner getting it right 78% of the time — or a 78/22 mixture of two types. The aggregate cannot tell them apart.
🟨 Static eval · Category summary
What the yellow category adds up to

Same methodological move. Differ chiefly in how wide the net is.

Lorè

Factorial, accidentally surfaces bimodality.

Fan

Broader battery, rationality scores.

MindGames

Broadest net, one number per model per capability.

What the category reliably does. Reproduces: more capable models track structure more closely. Surfaces: framing sensitivity.
What the category reliably doesn't. Ask whether the model is one reasoner, a mixture of types, or a pattern-matcher conditioning on unmeasured prompt features. The within-cell distribution is not the output it reports.
🟥 Dynamic / multi-agent evaluation
What the red category does

Put time back into the experiment. The unit becomes a trajectory, not a trial.

What iteration makes visible — theory of mind (beliefs matter only across time), norms of retaliation and forgiveness (time-indexed), equilibrium selection (coordination on an alternating BoS equilibrium requires repeated play).

Akata et al.

Iterate the game: same two players, many rounds.

Willis et al.

Iterate the strategies: population of LLM-authored strategies competes across generations.

🟥 Akata et al. · The asymmetry
Self-interest games vs. coordination games

Prisoner's Dilemma family

GPT-4 defects where theory says it should.

Retaliates on defection. Never forgives.

Pure grim trigger — rational for self, worse for the collective.

Battle of the Sexes

Picks its own preferred coordination point.

Refuses to alternate, even with an alternating partner.

Not selfishness. A failure of theory of mind.

5 models · 7 game families · 10 rounds · 1,224 games total. Methodologically the closest thing in this literature to Camerer's experimental-GT tradition.
🟥 Akata et al. · SCoT & the design tension
"Predict the partner first"

Prompt GPT-4 to predict the partner's next move before choosing its own. Coordination substantially improves; blind evaluators rate it as more human-like.

Unprompted GPT-4 in PD

Individually rational. Higher payoff via grim trigger.

SCoT-prompted GPT-4 in PD

More human-like. More forgiving, more cooperative.

"More human-like" is not the same as "more rational," and is sometimes its opposite. For joint welfare SCoT wins; for unilateral payoff maximisation against a defector, the unprompted model wins.
The field has not settled which should be the default evaluative criterion. That is a substantive question the metric silently decides.
🟥 Willis et al. · Moran tournament
LLMs author strategies; evolution picks among them

Rather than asking an LLM to play, ask it to write IPD strategies as Python. Enter them into a Moran-process tournament — an evolutionary simulation where successful strategies reproduce and unsuccessful ones drop out.

GPT-4o-authored strategies

Evolve toward aggression.

Claude-authored strategies

Evolve toward cooperation.

Read Willis as Axelrod 1984 rerun with LLM-authored strategies in place of hand-submitted ones. Same analytic ceiling.
10% action noise breaks coordination. Whether a single model could generate a diversity of noise-robust strategies — a mixture question — is implicit in the setup, and not analysed.
Interim synthesis
What the yellow + red categories give us
1
LLMs respond to game-theoretic structure. Payoff orderings theory says should matter do matter, for capable models. Lorè Fan Akata
2
LLMs are sensitive to framing. Context shifts behaviour at fixed payoffs — reminiscent of Tversky–Kahneman. Lorè
3
LLMs reproduce classical behavioural-economics effects directionally. Horton
What iteration (red) does not buy on its own: within-model heterogeneity. Akata reports model-level profiles; Willis reports model-level tendencies. 12,240 trajectories in Akata — never unpacked.
Part II · The shift
From aggregate means to patterned heterogeneity

Behavioural GT 1960s–1990s: aggregate means.

Cooperation at some fraction. Ultimatum rejections at some rate.

Then a question: is the aggregate hiding the structure?
Behind a 45% cooperation rate: some subjects cooperate 80%, others 15%, almost nobody at the mean.
The question is not what is the average behaviour, but what kinds of agents are in the room.
Part II · The canonical result
Fischbacher, Gächter & Fehr (2001) → the methods that followed

≈ 50%

Conditional cooperators — contribute proportionally to beliefs.

≈ 30%

Free-riders — contribute near zero regardless.

residual

Altruists, hump-shaped, inconsistent.

Recovering types from trajectories

DTW (Sakoe & Chiba 1978) + hierarchical / spectral clustering. Mixture models / HMMs.

Recovering intentions

Inverse RL, hierarchical inverse Q-learning. What reward was the agent pursuing?

This is the machinery. It has existed for decades. It made behavioural GT into an empirical science.
Part II · Diagnosis
The LLM literature is where behavioural GT was in 1996

Horton

Heterogeneity imposed by persona. Not recovered.

Lorè

Bimodal GPT-4. Reported, then moved past.

Akata

Heterogeneity cross-model only. 12,240 trajectories not unpacked.

Sun

"Heterogeneity" = users, not model behaviour.

Treat every trajectory as a unit. Ask: do they form clusters? Are the clusters stable across seeds, prompts, games? Is the latent structure a prompt artefact or a model property?
Not a criticism — a diagnosis. Each paper is well-executed in its own frame. The gap is at the field level.
🟪 Binz et al. · Centaur
The one partial counterexample — and what it still doesn't do

Llama 3.1 70B fine-tuned on Psych-101 (10.7M choices, 160 experiments, natural-language-expressible only). Generalizes across modified cover stories, new problem structures, and new domains (LSAT). The paper on this reading list most directly aligned with the course title.

Fig. 2c — the mixture signal

On the two-step task, Centaur produces a bimodal distribution over the model-based parameter: pure habit-learners, pure planners, mixtures in between. Matches the human distribution.

The authors: "Not merely the average participant, but the distribution over trajectories."

What Centaur still doesn't do

No persona / demographic inputs. Can't answer the Fischbacher question: are types stable across paradigms?

Psych-101 is cognitive psychology — no voting, survey, or negotiation data in the training distribution.

The field's default is still the aggregate. Fig. 2c is the one clear counterexample in this week's reading — and nobody has built on it.
Your questions this week, addressed
Two short answers — and pointers to the rest

Elena — how does Centaur align with the brain?

Fig. 4 of the Centaur paper. Run each model on the trials humans saw in a scanner; regress the fMRI signal onto the hidden-layer activations; compare fits. Centaur beats Llama, with alignment peaking in middle layers.

Fine-tuning on behavior reshaped the representations toward something more brain-like — not the stronger claim that Centaur is mechanistically a brain.

Anastasiia, Artur, Elena — does stated reasoning match action?

Fan's Ring-Network experiment is the closest existing answer: GPT-4 can correctly articulate the opponent's optimal move and still not best-respond to it. Fan labels these failure modes belief overlooked and belief modified.

No paper treats CoT-trace-vs-action as a primary measurement target. Group-project angle: collect chain-of-thought traces across a factorial and ask whether they predict the moves that follow.

Bridge to Jingyao · 🟩 Kozlowski & Evans
The validity apparatus itself, examined

We asked: does the behavioural-GT method transfer to LLMs?

They ask: does the social-science validity apparatus transfer at all?

Six reasons to worry

Bias Uniformity Atemporality Linguistic cultures Disembodiment Alien intelligence

Uniformity (their Figure 6): GPT-4 on the GSS has far lower variance than the human population. Either (a) a few types the prompt fails to separate, or (b) no within-subject variability at all. Same underlying question as Part II.
Two proposed paradigms — validate-then-simulate / simulate-then-validate. Careful, measured. Not "simulation is useless"; "it is technically difficult in specific ways."
Frontiers & what to bring
Two pointers and a handful of threads

Park et al. 2024 — 1,000 personas

Each simulated person prompted with their own 2-hour interview transcript.

85% test-retest with the real human on a survey battery.

Many-shot prompting as an alternative to fine-tuning.

Vong et al. 2024 — embodied

VLM trained on 65 hours of head-cam video from a 6–25-month-old child.

Grounds words to visual referents, not other words.

The remedy-direction for disembodiment.

Threads for discussion
★
Reasoning, restated through types. If GPT-4 is bimodal under identical prompts, do the two modes correspond to coherent strategic policies?
★
"Human-like" is a choice, not a default. SCoT makes the model more cooperative — and less rational. Which criterion is the field optimising for?
★
Multi-model replication is not optional. Single-model studies describe a specific system at a specific snapshot.
