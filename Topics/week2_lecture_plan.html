Recap · 1 of 3
This week: LLMs × strategic behavior

Four papers, one shared move: run LLMs as subjects in classical games and ask whether they play strategically.

Horton, Filippas & Manning (2023/26)

Large Language Models as Simulated Economic Agents — Homo Silicus.

Conceptual move: LLM-as-subject is a theory tool. Five classic experiments re-run with persona-prompted LLMs.

Lorè & Heydari (2024)

Strategic Behavior of LLMs: Game Structure vs. Contextual Framing.

Factorial design: 3 models × 4 games × 5 contexts × 300 runs. Finds GPT-4 plays near-bimodally.

Akata et al. (2025)

Playing Repeated Games with Large Language Models.

Behavioral-game-theory methodology applied to 5 LLMs × 7 game families. Key finding: GPT-4 is unforgiving in PD, fails coordination in Battle of Sexes.

Sun et al. (2025)

Game Theory Meets LLMs: A Systematic Survey.

Four-part taxonomy of the GT × LLM research field. Useful as a map of where things sit.

What ties them together: the LLM is the experimental subject; the game is the instrument; the question is whether LLM behaviour lines up with what we expect from strategic actors. This is a new research programme — the tools it borrows are mostly borrowed from our tradition.
Plan · 2 of 3
Today's plan — two halves

Part A — Foundations of GT & BGT

Strategic form, Nash equilibrium, dominant strategies. Prisoner's Dilemma, coordination games, Battle of Sexes. Repeated games and the Folk Theorem. Where behavioral GT diverged from classical GT: social preferences, reciprocity, fairness, forgiveness.

Why first: being firm in these concepts strengthens the social-science perspective you bring. These are also the fundamentals the LLM literature itself draws on — Akata cites the Folk Theorem, Lorè & Heydari cite social-preference theory, Horton frames everything around economic rationality. You cannot critique what you do not know.

Part B — Leverage: patterned heterogeneity

We don't just read the LLM literature — we ask how to extend it. The strategic way to do that is to ask: where does our background give us an edge?

Answer: 30 years of behavioral GT has settled a question the LLM literature is still aggregating past — populations in social dilemmas are patterned into stable, recoverable types, not noise around a mean. In the second half we review the four papers through that lens.

Flow: Bridge (here) → GT & BGT foundations deck → patterned-heterogeneity review of this week's four papers. Two transitions, one argument.
Framing · 3 of 3
The move: read to extend, not read to summarise

Everyone can summarise a paper. The interesting question is: where does it stop, and where could it go next?

Two unhelpful moves to avoid

Don't compete on their turf

We are not going to out-engineer an ML lab. "What if we tried GPT-4o instead of GPT-4" is not where our contribution lives.

Don't dismiss the whole programme

"LLMs aren't really reasoning" is a conversation-ender. The methodologically interesting questions are inside the programme, not outside it.

The helpful move
Leverage your inheritance. Behavioral game theory has spent 30 years building a toolkit for exactly the kinds of data these papers generate: mixture models, clustering, latent-variable methods, identifying assumptions about beliefs and intentions. The LLM literature is often a generation behind on this toolkit — not because the researchers are weaker, but because the methods come from a different tradition. Bringing that tradition forward is concrete, doable, and your comparative advantage.
Today's case study: patterned heterogeneity. After the foundations refresher, we'll walk through the four papers and ask whether any of them treats behavioral types the way the human literature has since Fischbacher, Gächter & Fehr (2001). Spoiler: none of them does — and one of them almost does without realising it.
