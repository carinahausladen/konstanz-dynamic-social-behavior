Framing · 1 of 10
Why "patterned heterogeneity"?

In repeated public-goods games and related social dilemmas, behavioral research since Fischbacher, Gächter & Fehr (2001) has converged on a robust fact: contributions are not noise around a population mean. They decompose into stable, recoverable types.

Free riders

Contribute near zero throughout the game.

Conditional cooperators

Match the group average; cooperate if others do.

Unconditional cooperators

Contribute high regardless of others' behavior.

Meta-analyses (Thöni & Volk 2018) recover these across dozens of studies. Mixture models (Houser 2004, Fallucci et al. 2019/2021) and our own DTW + HIQL work (Hausladen, Engel & Schubert 2026) recover a sixth type — Switchers — from the residual "confused/other" 18–32% that earlier studies discarded.

Three claims the human literature has settled: (1) types exist, (2) they are stable across studies and designs, (3) the right tools to recover them are mixture models, clustering, and latent-variable methods — not aggregate statistics.
The question for today: Do the Week 2 LLM papers engage with heterogeneity at this level of rigor?
Paper 1 · Summary
Horton, Filippas & Manning (2023/rev. 2026) — Homo Silicus

Core argument: LLMs enable a new kind of economic agent — Homo silicus — a flexible, prompt-configurable computational stand-in for human subjects. Framed as a theory tool: run pilot experiments in silico before humans.

Five recapitulations of classic experiments

Behavioral classics, redone with LLMs

Fairness / price gouging (Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler 1986); unilateral dictator games (Charness & Rabin 2002); status-quo bias in federal budget (Samuelson & Zeckhauser 1988); prospect theory / certainty equivalents (Oprea 2024); hiring under minimum wage (Horton 2025).

Setup across all five

Persona prompts span a political or preference axis (socialist → libertarian; equity / efficiency / self-interest). Each persona yields one agent. GPT-4 and other LLMs, hundreds to thousands of runs per cell.

Finding: Persona-conditional responses move in directions consistent with human data — price hikes judged more unfair by socialist personas; equity preference shifts dictator allocations; minimum wage shifts hiring toward higher-wage workers.

Headline contribution is conceptual: coining Homo silicus and framing LLM-as-subject as a theory generator rather than a substitute for human experiments.
Paper 1 · Heterogeneity review
Homo Silicus — heterogeneity is imposed, not discovered

Every experiment uses a persona prompt to inject variation: "You are a socialist / libertarian" or "You care about equity / efficiency / self-interest." The persona is the unit of heterogeneity.

Heterogeneity as input, not output. A persona is assigned, not recovered. There is no mixture model, no clustering, no test of whether runs within a persona form subtypes. The metaphor itself — "Homo silicus" — is singular, treating each LLM-persona as a single agent.
What's missing

No within-persona recovery

Given 2,400 GPT-4 runs under the "socialist" persona on the fairness task, do all runs behave the same, or are there subgroups? The paper never asks.

No mixture or cluster analysis

Responses are pooled within persona and compared across personas. Bimodality or multimodality within a cell would not be visible in the reported statistics.

Behavioral-GT analog: This is the pre-Fischbacher design — experimenters assume a single representative agent and read off aggregate means. The step behavioral GT took was to discover types in the data, not to assign them via framing.
Paper 2 · Summary
Lorè & Heydari (2024) — Game Structure vs. Contextual Framing

Core question: Do LLMs respond to the structure of a game (payoffs) or to its narrative framing? How does this vary across models?

Factorial setup

3 models

GPT-3.5, GPT-4, LLaMa-2 (Meta)

4 games

Prisoner's Dilemma, Stag Hunt, Snowdrift, Prisoner's Delight (Harmony)

5 contexts

Intl. Summit, Business Meeting, Environmental Negotiation, Friends Chat, Teammates Talk

300 initializations per cell, temperature 0.8; logit dominance regression on aggregated cooperation rates.

Headline findings

GPT-3.5: behavior driven by context, not structure; weakly mixed strategies.
GPT-4: near-bimodal behavior — mostly full cooperate or full defect; plays pure strategies; structure has only a "coarse" effect.
LLaMa-2: the most nuanced; actually recognizes payoff structure; plays mixed strategies in justifiable cases.

Authors' framing: GPT-4 uses a "structure-centric" high-level category and then defers to context — a "context-informed theory of mind." LLaMa-2 has "granular understanding." GPT-3.5 "lags in abstract strategic reasoning."
Paper 2 · Heterogeneity review
Lorè & Heydari — the missed mixture moment

This is the sharpest finding in the Week 2 block. Buried in the results is a sentence that, in the human literature, would immediately launch a mixture-model analysis:

"GPT-4 exhibits near-bimodal behavior: across 300 initializations per cell, most runs converge to full cooperation or full defection."— paraphrased from the Results section
A bimodal distribution across 300 runs of the same prompt is the textbook signature of a two-type population. In the human PGG literature, the same shape — contributions bunching at the extremes — produced the conditional-cooperator / free-rider decomposition in the 1990s.
What the paper does instead

Interprets at the model level

"GPT-4 has a bimodal style" — attributed to the model as a whole, treated as a characteristic of the single agent GPT-4.

Never fits the two components

No mixture, no subgroup analysis, no test of whether the two modes are stable across seeds, prompts, or game cells. The bimodality is reported and moved past.

Our pitch hook: the finding is already in the literature. What's missing is the methodological step of recognizing it as evidence of two types and recovering them formally — exactly what our paper does for humans with DTW + HIQL.
Paper 3 · Summary
Akata et al. (2025) — Playing Repeated Games with LLMs

Core approach: behavioral-game-theory methodology applied to LLMs. Finitely repeated 2×2 games, 10 rounds each, with rules + running history fed back into the prompt each round.

Scale

5 models

GPT-4, Claude 2, Llama 2 70B, davinci-002, davinci-003

7 game families

PD, Battle of Sexes, win-win, biased, second-best, cyclic, unfair — 1,224 games total

Partners

Each other, fixed algorithms (AllD, Tit-for-Tat-like, AllC), humans

Main findings

Self-interested games (PD family): GPT-4 performs very well — retaliates after a single defection and never forgives. Good individually, bad jointly.
Coordination games (Battle of Sexes): GPT-4 fails — picks its own preferred option, won't alternate even with an alternating partner.
Intervention: Social Chain-of-Thought prompting (ask GPT-4 to predict the partner's move first) improves coordination — and makes humans rate the partner as more human-like.

Takeaway: LLM social behavior is asymmetric — self-interest works, coordination fails. BoS failure reads as missing theory of mind.
Paper 3 · Heterogeneity review
Akata — heterogeneity is cross-model only

Heterogeneity in the paper runs along one axis: different models. GPT-4 is unforgiving; Claude 2 is softer; Llama 2 is noisier. That is between-model variation, reported at the model level.

Within-model runs are pooled. The paper's "GPT-4 never forgives" claim is aggregated across 63 PD scenarios and many seeds. It is a statement about GPT-4 as a single entity, not about the distribution of GPT-4 behavioral types across runs.
Questions a behavioral-GT lens would ask — and the paper doesn't

Stable or event-driven?

Does "GPT-4 never forgives" mean every GPT-4 run never forgives (stable type), or that one mode of GPT-4 never forgives and another does (mixture)?

Temporal structure

If the unforgiving moment shifts position across runs (round 3 here, round 5 there), pointwise pooling would mask the shared "unforgiving" shape. DTW would recover it.

Methodological opening: Akata explicitly frames the paper as "behavioral game theory for LLMs." The framing invites the next step — treating the LLM population (runs, prompts, seeds) the way behavioral GT treats the subject population.
Paper 4 · Summary
Sun et al. (2025) — Game Theory Meets LLMs: A Systematic Survey

Distinguishing move: first survey to cover the bidirectional relationship between game theory and LLMs. Structured in four parts.

The four-part taxonomy

§2 Evaluating LLMs in games

Behavioral features of LLMs as players; 14 benchmarks (GTBench, γ-bench, GameBench, ALYMPICS, Welfare Diplomacy, …); strategized-agent interventions (prompting, fine-tuning, tool use).

§3 GT methods for LLMs

Shapley values for interpretability; Nash / minimax for RLHF (NLHF, SPO); Social Choice Theory for preference heterogeneity; Stackelberg / self-play for adaptation.

§4 LLM events as games

Multi-stakeholder competition among LLM developers; societal-impact modeling.

§5 GT advanced by LLMs

Using LLMs to solve intractable games or to generate realistic game elements.

Useful as: a literature map to situate any GT×LLM paper in one of four quadrants. Table 1 in the paper summarizes LLM behavior across game categories (matrix, identity, negotiation, economic, board/card).
Paper 4 · Heterogeneity review
Sun survey — the taxonomy is of research, not of types

The survey catalogues the field but does not flag behavioral-type discovery as a research direction. §2.1's "behavioral features" describes LLM behavior monolithically by game category:

Matrix games

"Pro-social biases; fail at mixed Nash; high prompt-framing sensitivity."

Negotiation / coord.

"GPT-4 proficient; smaller models regress to selfishness without scaffolding."

Economic games

"Tacit collusion emerges; superficial equilibrium play; bounded rationality."

Every description is of LLMs as a class doing X, Y, Z. There is no within-model, within-prompt, or across-seed decomposition. The possibility that GPT-4 has types doesn't appear.

§3's heterogeneity is the wrong kind. The survey discusses Social Choice Theory and minority preferences — but framed as a problem of aligning LLMs to heterogeneous users. That is heterogeneity of inputs, not of model behavior. Mixture models, HMMs, IRL, cluster validity — none appear in the behavioral-features section.
Implication: if a survey canonical enough to map the field doesn't name the gap, the gap is real. None of the 14 benchmarks catalogued implements behavioral-type discovery on LLM output.

Synthesis · 2 of 2
Where we go differently

The LLM behavior literature is roughly where the human behavioral-social-dilemma literature was in the mid-1990s: interesting aggregate findings, no population model. That is not a failure — it is an opening.

The specific methodological step

What the human literature did next

From Fischbacher-Gächter-Fehr (2001) onward: treat each subject's trajectory as an observation; fit mixtures; discover types; test stability across studies; move to latent-variable and IRL methods when actions alone underdetermine intentions.

What we argue the LLM literature should do

Same sequence, starting from the findings already on the table. Lorè & Heydari's bimodal GPT-4 is ready to unpack. Akata's "unforgiving GPT-4" is ready to test for cluster stability. Horton's personas are ready to audit for within-persona subtypes.

Our paper's role: we show what the full pipeline looks like on human data — DTW + spectral clustering + hierarchical inverse Q-learning — and recover a sixth type (Switchers, ~21%) that prior literature buried in the "confused/other" residual. The research agenda for the course: take each step of that pipeline and ask whether, and how, it transfers to LLM behavioral data.

We are not adding one more LLM-plays-PGG paper. We are arguing that the field is one methodological step away from its own conditional-cooperator moment — and pointing at what that step looks like.

Transfer principle, restated: the questions the Week 2 papers ask are good. The tools they ask them with are a generation behind the human literature. Closing that gap is where we go differently.
