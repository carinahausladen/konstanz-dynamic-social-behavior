Week 3 · Humanities lens
What do we already know from studying humans?
Insights and methods from the social & cognitive sciences — and whether they transfer to LLMs

This week's three papers test LLMs on tasks that the social and cognitive sciences have studied in humans for decades: theory of mind, cooperation under social dilemmas, and behavioral prediction across cognitive tasks.

Before asking "what do LLMs do?", we ask: what do we already know from the human literature, and which methods can we bring?

Our comparative advantage is not in building better models. It is in knowing what questions to ask, what methods to apply, and what the answers mean — because we have studied this in humans first.

Cognitive science · 1 of 9
Theory of Mind: what cognitive science settled

Theory of Mind (ToM) is the ability to attribute mental states — beliefs, desires, intentions — to others. Developmental psychology mapped it with remarkable precision:

The Sally-Anne test (Wimmer & Perner 1983)

Sally puts a marble in her basket and leaves. Anne moves it to a box. Where will Sally look? Children under ~4 say "the box" (where it actually is). From age 4–5, they say "the basket" — they model Sally's false belief.

This is first-order ToM: I know what you believe.

Higher-order ToM

Second-order: I know what you think I believe. Develops ~age 6–7.

Third-order and beyond: I know what you think she thinks I believe. Measured in adults; varies by cognitive load and culture.

Strategic games require at least second-order ToM: "I cooperate because I think you think I'll cooperate."

Why this matters for the readings: When Binz (Centaur) predicts human behavior in cognitive tasks, the model inherits whatever ToM-like structure humans bring. When Willis tests whether LLMs cooperate, the question is whether their strategies reflect any level of ToM at all. Kozlowski asks whether LLM simulations of humans can be valid if the model lacks the developmental architecture that produces ToM in humans.
Cognitive science · 2 of 9
How cognitive science models human behavior

Before LLMs, cognitive science built formal models to predict human choices trial by trial. These are the benchmarks Centaur has to beat.

Bayesian cognitive models

Treat humans as approximate Bayesian inference engines: prior beliefs + evidence → posterior → action. Used in learning, perception, language. Griffiths & Tenenbaum (2006).

Reinforcement learning models

Q-learning, Rescorla-Wagner: humans learn action values from reward feedback. Well-tested in multi-armed bandits — exactly the tasks in Psych-101. Sutton & Barto (2018).

Drift-diffusion models

Model the process of deciding: evidence accumulates noisily until a threshold is reached. Predict both choices and response times. Ratcliff & McKoon (2008).

The evaluation framework cognitive science uses

Model comparison via AIC / BIC / log-likelihood — the exact metrics Centaur reports. A new model is only better if it predicts held-out choices more accurately than the existing best model, with fewer free parameters.

Transfer question: Centaur is evaluated against these cognitive models and wins. But what does it mean to "beat" a Bayesian model? It means the LLM captures variance in human behavior that the cognitive model's assumptions miss — which raises the question: what is it capturing? The cognitive model tells you (e.g. "learning rate"), the LLM doesn't.
Cognitive science · 3 of 9
The Psych-101 dataset as a cumulative achievement

Centaur's training set — Psych-101 — is not just "data." It is the cumulative output of decades of experimental cognitive science: 160 experiments, 60,092 participants, 10.7 million choices, transcribed into natural language.

What's in it

Multi-armed bandits, risky choice, memory tasks, supervised learning, Markov decision processes. Each experiment was originally designed to test a specific cognitive theory — Psych-101 flattens them into a shared format.

What that flattening gains and loses

Gains: a single model trained across all tasks. Generalization to new paradigms. Cross-domain transfer.

Loses: the original theoretical structure. Each experiment was designed to isolate a cognitive mechanism — pooling them together dissolves that isolation.

Humanities insight: Experimental paradigms in cognitive science are not just data-collection instruments. They are theoretical commitments — each one operationalizes a specific concept (working memory, risk preference, exploration). When Centaur treats them as interchangeable input, it gains prediction power but loses the ability to say why someone chose what they chose. This is a feature/bug tradeoff that cognitive scientists are still debating.
Methodology · 4 of 9
The validity framework: 80 years of social science methodology

When social scientists run experiments on human subjects, they evaluate results against a mature validity framework. Kozlowski & Evans ask whether this framework transfers to LLM-as-subject.

Construct validity

Does the measure actually capture the concept it claims to capture? If we ask an LLM "how fair is this price?", is it measuring the LLM's fairness judgment — or is it reproducing the most common answer from training data?

Internal validity

Can we attribute the result to the manipulation, not a confound? In human experiments: randomization. In LLM experiments: prompt variation could be a confound (temperature, phrasing, order effects).

External validity

Do results generalize to other populations, contexts, times? Human experiments face the WEIRD problem (Henrich et al. 2010): samples are Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic. LLMs inherit this bias through training data.

Ecological validity

Does the lab setting resemble the real-world context? Humans in labs behave differently than in life. LLMs have no real-world context — everything is a "lab."

The transfer question: Which validity types strengthen for LLMs (e.g. perfect experimental control, unlimited sample size) and which collapse (e.g. ecological validity, construct validity when training data contains the answer)?
Methodology · 5 of 9
What the replication crisis taught us — and why LLM research should listen

Psychology's replication crisis (2011–present) produced hard-won methodological lessons. The LLM behavior literature is re-learning some of them.

Lesson 1: Researcher degrees of freedom

In human experiments, flexible analytic choices inflate false positives (Simmons et al. 2011). In LLM experiments, prompt engineering is the equivalent: try enough prompt variants and you'll find one that gives you the result you want.

Lesson 2: Measurement matters more than you think

Psychometrics teaches that a measure must be reliable (same result on re-test) and valid (captures what you claim). LLM experiments rarely report test-retest reliability across seeds — the same prompt can produce different behavior at different temperatures.

Lesson 3: Effect sizes, not just significance

"GPT-4 cooperates significantly more than chance" tells you nothing about how much more, or whether it matters. Human behavioral research learned to demand effect sizes and confidence intervals.

Lesson 4: Pre-registration

Specify your hypothesis and analysis before seeing data. Virtually no LLM behavior study is pre-registered. The field is where psychology was in 2005.

The humanities contribution here is not critique — it is prevention. We already know what goes wrong when these norms are absent. Bringing them into LLM research early saves a decade of post-hoc corrections.
Cooperation · 6 of 9
Axelrod's tournaments: what we learned about cooperation

In 1984, Robert Axelrod ran what might be the most influential computer tournament in social science: scholars submitted strategies for the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma. The winner was Tit-for-Tat — the simplest possible strategy.

Axelrod's four properties of successful strategies

1. Nice

Never defect first. Start cooperative.

2. Retaliatory

Punish defection immediately — don't let exploitation go unanswered.

3. Forgiving

After retaliating, return to cooperation if the other does. Don't hold grudges.

4. Clear

Be predictable. Let the opponent understand your strategy so they can adapt.

Direct connection to Willis et al.: Willis runs Axelrod's experiment with LLM-generated strategies. The key question is whether LLM strategies exhibit these four properties — and whether the ones that do outperform the ones that don't. Akata (Week 2) already showed GPT-4 is retaliatory but not forgiving. In Axelrod's framework, that predicts failure in evolutionary competition. Does Willis's data confirm this?
Humanities insight: Axelrod showed that successful strategies are simple. The most complex, cleverly-designed strategies lost. LLMs can generate arbitrarily complex strategies — the question is whether complexity helps or hurts, exactly as Axelrod predicted.
Cooperation · 7 of 9
Ostrom's design principles: how humans actually sustain cooperation

While Axelrod studied strategy, Elinor Ostrom (Nobel 2009) studied institutions — the rules, norms, and structures that real communities build to sustain cooperation in social dilemmas without external enforcement.

Eight design principles for long-enduring commons (Ostrom 1990)

Relevant for LLM multi-agent systems

1. Clear group boundaries — who is in, who is out.

3. Collective-choice arrangements — those affected by rules can modify them.

4. Monitoring — behavior is observable.

5. Graduated sanctions — punishments escalate, not all-or-nothing.

Hard to transfer to LLMs

2. Local conditions — rules adapted to specific contexts. LLMs have no local context.

6. Conflict resolution — cheap, accessible, local. LLMs can't "resolve" disputes.

7. Autonomy — external authorities respect the group's right to organize. Who is the "authority" over LLM agents?

8. Nested enterprises — governance at multiple scales. Requires institutional depth LLMs don't have.

The gap Willis exposes: Willis tests populations of LLM-generated strategies in evolutionary competition — but with no institutions, no communication, no norm formation. In Ostrom's framework, that is a commons without governance. The finding that aggressive strategies dominate (for ChatGPT-4o) is exactly what Ostrom predicted for ungoverned commons. The question is whether LLM agents could build institutional structures — and that is a research frontier.
Cooperation · 8 of 9
Cross-cultural research: cooperation is not universal

One of the most robust findings from behavioral social science: cooperation norms vary dramatically across cultures. This has direct implications for using LLMs as simulated subjects.

Henrich et al. (2001, 2010)

Ultimatum and public-goods games played in 15 small-scale societies on five continents. Offers ranged from 15% (Machiguenga) to 58% (Lamalera). No society played the Nash equilibrium. The degree of "fairness" tracked market integration and community size — not individual rationality.

The WEIRD problem

Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan (2010): 96% of psychology subjects come from WEIRD societies, which are statistical outliers on cooperation, fairness, and punishment. LLMs are trained predominantly on English-language, Western-origin text — they inherit the same bias, possibly amplified.

For Kozlowski & Evans: if LLM-simulated subjects reproduce human behavior, whose human behavior? The answer is almost certainly WEIRD behavior. Cross-cultural research shows this is not universal. Any claim that "LLMs behave like humans" needs to specify which humans — and the social science literature tells us that matters enormously for cooperation, fairness, and punishment norms.
For Willis: Claude 3.5 Sonnet evolves toward cooperation, ChatGPT-4o toward aggression. Is this a difference in "strategic ability," or a difference in which cultural norms each model internalized from its training data? Cross-cultural research suggests the latter is at least as plausible.
Synthesis · 9 of 9
The humanities methods toolkit: what transfers and what doesn't
Methods that transfer directly

Psychometrics

Test-retest reliability, measurement invariance across conditions, factor analysis of behavioral dimensions. Apply directly to LLM outputs across seeds/prompts.

Computational cognitive models

Bayesian learners, RL models, drift-diffusion. Use as benchmarks — not as the model itself, but as the standard any new approach must beat.

Behavioral type discovery

Mixture models, DTW clustering, latent-variable methods. Last week's toolkit, applied to LLM behavioral data. This is where patterned heterogeneity lives.

Methods that need adaptation

Experimental design

Randomization, counterbalancing, control conditions — all apply, but the "subject" doesn't fatigue, learn across sessions, or have demand characteristics in the usual sense. New confounds (prompt sensitivity, temperature) need new controls.

Survey methodology

Question wording effects, response scales, acquiescence bias — all documented in humans, all present in LLMs but for different reasons (training distribution vs. cognitive bias).

Methods that fundamentally don't transfer

Ethnography / qualitative methods

Participant observation, thick description, lived experience. LLMs have no lived experience. You cannot do ethnography on a language model.

Physiological measures

fMRI, skin conductance, eye tracking — embodied signals that ground cognition in biology. Centaur shows its representations align with fMRI, but it has no body producing those signals.

Bottom line: the humanities contribution to LLM behavioral research is not "we read more carefully." It is a concrete toolkit — validity frameworks, measurement theory, type discovery, cognitive benchmarks, cross-cultural baselines — developed over decades of studying the thing LLMs are being asked to simulate. The papers that use this toolkit will be the ones that last.
