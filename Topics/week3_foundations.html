Theory of Mind · 1 of 2
Theory of Mind: reasoning about others' beliefs

Theory of Mind (ToM) is the ability to attribute mental states -- beliefs, desires, intentions -- to other agents, and to understand that those states can differ from your own and from reality.

First-order ToM

"I believe that you believe X."

Example: In the classic Sally-Anne test, Sally puts a marble in a basket and leaves. Anne moves it to a box. Where will Sally look? A child with first-order ToM says "the basket" -- Sally has a false belief.

Higher-order ToM

"I believe that you believe that I believe X."

Example: In a strategic game, you don't just model your opponent's belief about the world -- you model their belief about your strategy. This is exactly what iterated best response and level-k reasoning require.

Why this matters for games: Nash equilibrium requires that each player correctly models the other's strategy. Behavioral GT showed humans often don't go beyond 1-2 levels of reasoning (Camerer, Ho & Chong's level-k model). The question for LLMs is: how deep does their strategic reasoning actually go?
Key distinction: ToM is about modeling another agent's mental state, not about being smart in general. A model can be excellent at logic puzzles but fail completely at predicting what someone with a false belief would do.
Theory of Mind · 2 of 2
Can LLMs model others' beliefs?

This is one of the most contested questions in AI research right now. The answer depends heavily on what you mean by "model."

1
Pattern-matching ToM: LLMs trained on enough text about false-belief tasks can pass them in prompt form. GPT-4 passes standard Sally-Anne variants. But is this reasoning about beliefs, or pattern-matching over stories that describe belief reasoning?
2
Fragile generalization: When researchers modify the surface features of ToM tasks (e.g., transparent containers instead of opaque ones), LLM performance often collapses. Humans -- even young children -- handle such variations effortlessly.
3
Functional vs. genuine ToM: A pragmatic stance says: if a model behaves as if it has ToM in the contexts that matter, that's enough for scientific purposes. You don't need to resolve the philosophical question to do useful behavioral science.
Connection to the readings: Binz et al. (Centaur) sidestep the ToM debate entirely -- they don't ask if the model has ToM, they fine-tune it to predict human behavior, including in tasks that require ToM-like reasoning. Willis et al. are relevant because their LLM-generated strategies must implicitly model what opponents will do -- a functional form of strategic ToM. Kozlowski & Evans flag ToM-like failures as a core validity concern: if an LLM can't represent how a specific demographic group would reason, the simulation breaks down.
Evolutionary Game Theory
Populations, fitness, and the Moran process

Classical GT asks: what would a rational agent do? Evolutionary GT asks a different question: in a population of agents playing strategies, which strategies survive?

Core idea

Agents don't "choose" strategies by reasoning. Instead, strategies that earn higher fitness (payoff) reproduce more frequently. Over time, successful strategies spread through the population.

No rationality assumption needed -- just differential reproduction.

The Moran process

A specific evolutionary model for finite populations:

1. Each round, one agent is selected to reproduce proportional to fitness.

2. Its offspring replaces a randomly chosen agent.

3. Repeat until one strategy fixates (takes over) or goes extinct.

This is exactly what Willis et al. use.

Key concept: Evolutionary Stable Strategy (ESS)

A strategy is an ESS if, once it dominates the population, no rare mutant strategy can invade. Think of it as the evolutionary analogue of Nash equilibrium -- but defined over populations, not individual choices. In the Prisoner's Dilemma, Defect is the only ESS in a one-shot game. But in repeated PD with memory, strategies like Tit-for-Tat can be stable.

Why this matters for Willis et al.: They let GPT-4o and Claude each generate many IPD strategies, then run a Moran process tournament. The question is not "what strategy does the LLM play?" but "what ecosystem of strategies does the LLM produce, and which ones survive selection pressure?"
Multi-Agent Dynamics
From agents to populations

Week 2 focused on individual LLM behavior in games. This week, the unit of analysis shifts: we care about what happens when many agents interact over time.

1
Strategy diversity matters. A single agent's strategy tells you little about population dynamics. Willis et al. find that GPT-4o generates strategies that individually look reasonable but collectively evolve toward mutual defection. The population-level outcome is not predictable from any single strategy.
2
Emergence. Population behavior is not the average of individual behaviors -- it emerges from interactions between strategies. A population of mostly-cooperators with a few aggressive exploiters behaves very differently from a population of moderate cooperators.
3
Selection pressure as a test. Asking "what strategy does an LLM play?" is one thing. Asking "what strategies does an LLM produce that survive evolutionary competition?" is a much harder test -- and more relevant if you care about multi-agent deployment.
The punchline from Willis et al.: GPT-4o-generated strategies evolve toward a competitive, aggressive equilibrium. Claude 3.5 Sonnet-generated strategies evolve toward cooperative ones. This is not about a single play -- it's about what kind of strategic ecosystem each model's training produces. The Moran process is the tool that reveals this.
Technical Foundations
Fine-tuning LLMs: what QLoRA is and why it matters

A pretrained LLM (like Llama 3.1 70B) has general language abilities. Fine-tuning adapts it to a specific task by continuing training on task-specific data. The problem: 70 billion parameters are expensive to update.

QLoRA in three steps
1
Quantize the base model. Compress the 70B parameters from 16-bit to 4-bit precision. This cuts memory by ~4x, making training possible on fewer GPUs. The base weights are frozen -- they never change.
2
Attach low-rank adapters (LoRA). Instead of updating all parameters, inject small trainable matrices into each layer. These adapters have very few parameters -- in Centaur's case, roughly 0.15% of the total model. Only these adapters are trained.
3
Train the adapters on your task data. The adapters learn to steer the frozen base model's behavior. At inference time, the adapter outputs are added to the base model's computations -- no extra latency.

Why this matters for Centaur

Binz et al. didn't build a cognitive model from scratch. They took a general-purpose LLM and fine-tuned it on human behavioral data. The claim is that the LLM's pretrained representations already encode something useful about human cognition -- QLoRA just steers it toward predicting specific behaviors.

The efficiency argument

Training 0.15% of parameters on 10.7M human choices is radically different from training a model from scratch on the same data. The base model brings prior knowledge from its pretraining corpus -- arguably including implicit knowledge of how people behave.

Centaur Deep Dive
Foundation models for cognition

Centaur is not another "let's prompt GPT and see if it acts human." It's a fundamentally different approach: train a model on actual human behavioral data and evaluate it as a predictive model of cognition.

The Psych-101 dataset

160 experiments spanning decision-making, memory, perception, reasoning, and more. 60K participants. 10.7M individual choices. Each experiment is converted to a text format the LLM can process: task description + trial-level stimuli + response.

Three levels of evaluation

Behavioral: Does it predict human choices trial-by-trial?

Generalization: Does it predict behavior in experiments it was never trained on?

Neural: Do its internal representations correlate with fMRI activation patterns?

Prediction vs. Roleplay
Critical distinction: Centaur is not "roleplaying" a human. Roleplay (what Week 2 papers mostly did) means prompting an LLM to behave as if it were a person. Centaur is trained to predict what a person actually did. The difference matters: roleplay reflects the model's priors about how people behave; prediction reflects the actual distribution of human behavior in the training data.
The strong claim: If an LLM fine-tuned on behavioral data also shows neural alignment with fMRI data (without ever being trained on brain data), this suggests the model has learned something about the computational structure of human cognition -- not just surface-level response patterns. This is Binz et al.'s most provocative finding.
Methodology
LLM-as-subject: four threats to validity

Kozlowski & Evans provide the methodological counterweight to the enthusiasm. When researchers use LLMs as stand-ins for human subjects, four things can go wrong:

1. Training-data contamination

If the LLM was trained on descriptions of the experiment you're running, it may reproduce known results rather than simulate actual reasoning. You're measuring the literature, not the phenomenon.

Ask: Could GPT have seen the Prisoner's Dilemma results in its training data?

2. WEIRD population bias

LLMs are trained predominantly on English-language, Western, educated, industrialized, rich, democratic text. When asked to simulate a rural Indian farmer or a Japanese teenager, the model draws on stereotypes, not lived experience.

Ask: Whose behavior is the LLM actually modeling?

3. Persona fidelity

You can prompt an LLM to "be" a 65-year-old retired teacher, but does the model maintain that persona consistently across a multi-trial experiment? Evidence suggests personas drift and interact unpredictably with task demands.

Ask: Is the persona stable enough to serve as a unit of analysis?

4. Measurement equivalence

Human subjects answer Likert scales, make timed decisions, show order effects. LLMs process text tokens. Are you measuring the same construct when you run the "same" experiment on both? Often the answer is no.

Ask: Would a psychometrician accept this as the same measure?

Kozlowski & Evans are not dismissing the programme. They're building a framework for when simulation evidence is credible and when it isn't. This is the kind of methodological infrastructure that any "LLM as subject" study should engage with.
Reading Guide
What to watch for in each reading

Binz et al. -- Centaur

Ask yourself:

How much work is the base LLM doing vs. the fine-tuning? If you fine-tuned a random model (not a language model) on Psych-101, would you get similar predictions?

What does "neural alignment" actually show? Correlation with fMRI is striking, but is the model learning the right thing or just something that correlates?

Where does Centaur fail? The paper is honest about this -- look for the failure modes.

Kozlowski & Evans

Ask yourself:

Which of the four validity threats is hardest to overcome? Which could be fixed with better methods, and which are fundamental?

How do the Week 2 papers (Horton, Akata, etc.) fare against this framework? Would they pass?

Is there a version of "LLM as subject" that the authors would endorse? What would it look like?

Willis et al.

Ask yourself:

Why do GPT-4o and Claude evolve differently? Is this about training data, RLHF, or something else?

Does generating strategies as code change the game vs. generating moves in natural language (as in Akata et al.)?

What would happen if you mixed GPT-4o and Claude strategies in the same Moran process? Who wins?

Across all three: Notice that each paper makes a different bet on what LLMs are -- a cognitive model (Binz), a simulation tool (Kozlowski & Evans), a strategy generator (Willis). These are not the same claim. Part of your job as a reader is to figure out which framing is doing the most honest work.
