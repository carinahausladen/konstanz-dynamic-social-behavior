Theory of Mind · Concept
Theory of Mind: reasoning about others' beliefs

Theory of Mind (ToM) is the ability to attribute mental states — beliefs, desires, intentions — to other agents, and to understand that those states can differ from your own and from reality. Developmental psychology mapped it with remarkable precision:

First-order ToM

"I believe that you believe X."

Sally-Anne test (Wimmer & Perner 1983): Sally puts a marble in her basket and leaves. Anne moves it to a box. Where will Sally look? Children under ~4 say "the box" (where it actually is). From age 4–5, they say "the basket" — they model Sally's false belief.

Higher-order ToM

Second-order (~age 6–7): "I know what you think I believe."

Third-order and beyond: measured in adults; varies by cognitive load and culture.

Strategic games require at least second-order ToM: "I cooperate because I think you think I'll cooperate."

Higher-order ToM for strategic games

Camerer, Ho & Chong's level-k model formalizes this: a level-0 player randomizes, a level-1 player best-responds to level-0, a level-2 player best-responds to level-1, and so on. Behavioral GT showed humans rarely go beyond 1–2 levels of reasoning. The question for LLMs: how deep does their strategic reasoning actually go?

Key distinction: ToM is about modeling another agent's mental state, not about being smart in general. A model can be excellent at logic puzzles but fail completely at predicting what someone with a false belief would do.
Theory of Mind · LLMs
Can LLMs model others' beliefs?

This is one of the most contested questions in AI research. The answer depends heavily on what you mean by "model."

1
Pattern-matching ToM: LLMs trained on enough text about false-belief tasks can pass them in prompt form. GPT-4 passes standard Sally-Anne variants. But is this reasoning about beliefs, or pattern-matching over stories that describe belief reasoning?
2
Fragile generalization: When researchers modify the surface features of ToM tasks (e.g., transparent containers instead of opaque ones), LLM performance often collapses. Humans — even young children — handle such variations effortlessly.
3
Functional vs. genuine ToM: A pragmatic stance says: if a model behaves as if it has ToM in the contexts that matter, that's sufficient for scientific purposes. You don't need to resolve the philosophical question to do useful behavioral science.
The humanities insight: Developmental psychology mapped ToM with precision — false-belief tasks at age 4, second-order ToM at 6–7, increasingly recursive reasoning into adulthood. LLMs skip the developmental architecture entirely: no embodiment, no social interaction, no gradual construction of a self-other distinction. They arrive at pattern-level competence without the process that produces it in humans. Kozlowski & Evans flag this as a core validity concern: if an LLM lacks the developmental architecture that produces ToM, can it faithfully simulate people who have it?
Connection to all three papers: Centaur sidesteps the ToM debate — it doesn't ask if the model has ToM, it fine-tunes to predict human behavior, including in tasks requiring ToM-like reasoning. Willis's LLM-generated strategies must implicitly model opponents — a functional form of strategic ToM. Kozlowski asks whether LLM simulations can be valid without genuine ToM.
Cognitive Science · Benchmarks
Computational cognitive models: what Centaur must beat

Before LLMs, cognitive science built formal models to predict human choices trial by trial. These are the benchmarks Centaur has to outperform.

Bayesian cognitive models

Treat humans as approximate Bayesian inference engines: prior beliefs + evidence = posterior = action. Used in learning, perception, language. Griffiths & Tenenbaum (2006).

Reinforcement learning models

Q-learning, Rescorla-Wagner: humans learn action values from reward feedback. Well-tested in multi-armed bandits — exactly the tasks in Psych-101. Sutton & Barto (2018).

Drift-diffusion models

Model the process of deciding: evidence accumulates noisily until a threshold is reached. Predict both choices and response times. Ratcliff & McKoon (2008).

How cognitive science evaluates models

Model comparison via AIC / BIC / log-likelihood — the exact metrics Centaur reports. A new model is only better if it predicts held-out choices more accurately than the existing best model, penalized for complexity (free parameters).

Transfer question: Centaur beats these cognitive models on prediction. But what does it mean to "beat" a Bayesian model? The cognitive model tells you why (e.g. "learning rate is 0.3, exploration bonus is 0.1"). The LLM doesn't. You gain predictive accuracy but lose mechanistic interpretability. Is that a trade you want to make? The answer depends on whether you're doing engineering (predict) or science (explain).
Technical Foundations · Centaur
QLoRA fine-tuning and the Psych-101 dataset
QLoRA in three steps
1
Quantize the base model. Compress Llama 3.1 70B from 16-bit to 4-bit precision. This cuts memory ~4x. The base weights are frozen — they never change.
2
Attach low-rank adapters (LoRA). Inject small trainable matrices into each layer — roughly 0.15% of total parameters. Only these adapters are trained.
3
Train the adapters on Psych-101: 160 experiments, 60,092 participants, 10.7 million choices, transcribed into natural language. At inference, adapter outputs are added to the base model's computations.

What Psych-101 represents

Not just "data" — the cumulative output of decades of experimental cognitive science. Multi-armed bandits, risky choice, memory, supervised learning, Markov decision processes. Each experiment was originally designed to test a specific cognitive theory.

What flattening gains and loses

Gains: one model across all tasks, cross-domain transfer, generalization to new paradigms.

Loses: the original theoretical structure. Pooling dissolves the isolation each experiment was designed to provide.

Prediction vs. roleplay: Centaur is not "roleplaying" a human. Roleplay (Week 2's approach) means prompting an LLM to behave as if it were a person. Centaur is trained to predict what a person actually did. Roleplay reflects the model's priors about how people behave; prediction reflects the actual distribution of human behavior in training data. This distinction is critical for evaluating every paper this week.
Methodology · Validity
The validity framework: 80 years of social science methodology

Construct validity

Does the measure capture the concept it claims? If we ask an LLM "how fair is this price?", is it measuring a fairness judgment or reproducing the most common answer from training data?

Internal validity

Can we attribute the result to the manipulation? In LLM experiments: prompt variation, temperature, and phrasing are confounds that need new controls.

External validity

Do results generalize? Human experiments face the WEIRD problem (Henrich et al. 2010). LLMs inherit this bias through training data — possibly amplified.

Ecological validity

Does the setting resemble real-world context? Humans in labs behave differently than in life. LLMs have no real-world context — everything is a "lab."

Replication crisis lessons LLM research must learn

Prompt engineering = researcher degrees of freedom

Simmons et al. (2011) showed flexible analytic choices inflate false positives. Try enough prompt variants and you'll find one that gives the result you want. Pre-registration is the cure — virtually no LLM behavior study uses it.

Measurement reliability

Psychometrics demands test-retest reliability. LLM experiments rarely report it across seeds. The same prompt can produce different behavior at different temperatures. The field is where psychology was in ~2005.

Which validity types strengthen for LLMs (perfect experimental control, unlimited sample size) and which collapse (ecological validity, construct validity when training data contains the answer)? This is the core analytical question Kozlowski & Evans pose.
Kozlowski & Evans · Four Threats
LLM-as-subject: four threats to validity

Kozlowski & Evans provide the methodological counterweight to the enthusiasm. When researchers use LLMs as stand-ins for human subjects, four things can go wrong:

1. Training-data contamination

If the LLM was trained on descriptions of the experiment you're running, it may reproduce known results rather than simulate reasoning. You're measuring the literature, not the phenomenon.

Diagnostic: Could GPT have seen these results in its training data?

2. WEIRD population bias

LLMs are trained predominantly on English-language, Western text. When asked to simulate a rural Indian farmer, the model draws on stereotypes, not lived experience.

Diagnostic: Whose behavior is the LLM actually modeling?

3. Persona fidelity

You can prompt an LLM to "be" a 65-year-old retired teacher, but does the model maintain that persona consistently across a multi-trial experiment? Evidence suggests personas drift.

Diagnostic: Is the persona stable enough to serve as a unit of analysis?

4. Measurement equivalence

Human subjects answer Likert scales, make timed decisions, show order effects. LLMs process text tokens. Are you measuring the same construct?

Diagnostic: Would a psychometrician accept this as the same measure?

Kozlowski & Evans are not dismissing the programme. They are building a framework for when simulation evidence is credible and when it isn't. This is the kind of methodological infrastructure that any "LLM as subject" study — including Centaur and Willis — should engage with.
Evolutionary Game Theory
Populations, fitness, and the Moran process

Classical GT asks: what would a rational agent do? Evolutionary GT asks: in a population of agents playing strategies, which strategies survive?

Core idea

Agents don't "choose" strategies by reasoning. Strategies that earn higher fitness (payoff) reproduce more frequently. Over time, successful strategies spread. No rationality assumption needed — just differential reproduction.

The Moran process

A specific evolutionary model for finite populations:

1. Each round, one agent reproduces proportional to fitness.

2. Its offspring replaces a randomly chosen agent.

3. Repeat until one strategy fixates (takes over) or goes extinct.

This is exactly what Willis et al. use.

Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS)

A strategy is an ESS if, once dominant, no rare mutant can invade — the evolutionary analogue of Nash equilibrium, but defined over populations. In one-shot PD, Defect is the only ESS. But in repeated PD with memory, strategies like Tit-for-Tat can be stable.

Why this matters for Willis: They let GPT-4o and Claude each generate many IPD strategies, then run a Moran process tournament. The question is not "what strategy does the LLM play?" but "what ecosystem of strategies does the LLM produce, and which ones survive selection pressure?" Population-level behavior is not the average of individual behaviors — it emerges from interactions between strategies.
Cooperation · Axelrod
Axelrod's tournaments: the most influential cooperation experiment

In 1984, Robert Axelrod ran what might be the most influential computer tournament in social science: scholars submitted strategies for the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma. The winner was Tit-for-Tat — the simplest possible strategy.

Four properties of successful strategies

1. Nice

Never defect first. Start cooperative.

2. Retaliatory

Punish defection immediately — don't let exploitation go unanswered.

3. Forgiving

After retaliating, return to cooperation if the other does. Don't hold grudges.

4. Clear

Be predictable. Let the opponent understand your strategy so they can adapt.

Direct link: Willis = Axelrod 2.0 with LLM-generated strategies. The key question is whether LLM strategies exhibit these four properties — and whether the ones that do outperform the ones that don't. Akata (Week 2) already showed GPT-4 is retaliatory but not forgiving. In Axelrod's framework, that predicts failure in evolutionary competition: grudge-holders get trapped in mutual defection spirals.
Humanities insight on complexity: Axelrod's deeper finding was that successful strategies are simple. The most complex, cleverly-designed strategies lost. LLMs can generate arbitrarily complex Python code strategies — the question is whether complexity helps or hurts, exactly as Axelrod predicted.
Cooperation · Ostrom & Cross-Cultural
Ostrom's design principles and the WEIRD problem

While Axelrod studied strategy, Elinor Ostrom (Nobel 2009) studied institutions — the rules, norms, and structures that real communities build to sustain cooperation without external enforcement.

Principles that transfer to LLM multi-agent

Clear boundaries — who is in the system.

Monitoring — behavior is observable.

Graduated sanctions — punishments escalate, not all-or-nothing.

Collective-choice arrangements — those affected by rules can modify them.

Principles that don't transfer

Local adaptation — LLMs have no local context.

Conflict resolution — requires cheap, accessible, local mechanisms.

Autonomy / nested governance — requires institutional depth LLMs don't have.

Willis's results as ungoverned commons: Willis tests populations with no institutions, no communication, no norm formation. In Ostrom's framework, that is a commons without governance. The finding that aggressive strategies dominate (for GPT-4o) is exactly what Ostrom predicted for ungoverned commons.
Whose cooperation norms did the LLM learn?

Henrich et al. (2001, 2010) showed cooperation norms vary dramatically across cultures — from 15% offers (Machiguenga) to 58% (Lamalera) in ultimatum games. No society played Nash. Claude cooperating vs. GPT-4o being aggressive may reflect different cultural training data, not different strategic ability. Cross-cultural research says this matters enormously — and should be tested, not assumed.

Synthesis · Methods
The humanities methods toolkit: what transfers and what doesn't
Transfers directly

Psychometrics

Test-retest reliability, measurement invariance across conditions, factor analysis of behavioral dimensions. Apply directly to LLM outputs across seeds and prompts.

Cognitive benchmarks

Bayesian learners, RL models, drift-diffusion. Use as the standard any new approach must beat — exactly as Centaur does.

Behavioral type discovery

Mixture models, DTW clustering, latent-variable methods. Week 2's toolkit, applied to LLM behavioral data. This is where patterned heterogeneity lives.

Needs adaptation

Experimental design

Randomization, counterbalancing, control conditions all apply, but the "subject" doesn't fatigue, learn across sessions, or show demand characteristics in the usual sense. New confounds (prompt sensitivity, temperature) need new controls.

Survey methodology

Question wording effects, response scales, acquiescence bias — all documented in humans, all present in LLMs but for different reasons (training distribution vs. cognitive bias).

Fundamentally doesn't transfer

Ethnography / qualitative methods

Participant observation, thick description, lived experience. LLMs have no lived experience. You cannot do ethnography on a language model.

Physiological measures

fMRI, skin conductance, eye tracking. Centaur's representations align with fMRI, but it has no body producing those signals.

Bottom line: the humanities contribution is a concrete toolkit — validity frameworks, measurement theory, type discovery, cognitive benchmarks, cross-cultural baselines — not just critique. The papers that use this toolkit will be the ones that last.
Synthesis · Research questions
Research question seeds: where human insight meets LLM research

Centaur (Binz et al.)

Can behavioral-type discovery (Week 2's DTW clustering) be applied to Centaur's predictions to find where it systematically diverges from human types? If Centaur predicts average behavior well but misses specific types (risk-seekers, explorers), that reveals what the fine-tuning actually learned.

Kozlowski & Evans

Can psychometric measurement invariance tests be used to formally diagnose when LLM-as-subject breaks? Measurement invariance (same factor structure across groups) is a standard tool — apply it to LLM vs. human response patterns. Where invariance fails, the simulation is not measuring the same construct.

Willis et al.

Run Axelrod's four-property diagnostic on LLM-generated strategies: are the winning strategies nice, retaliatory, forgiving, clear? Does Ostrom's framework predict which multi-agent configurations sustain cooperation? Test whether adding communication channels or graduated sanctions shifts the evolutionary outcome.

Cross-cutting question
Apply the patterned-heterogeneity lens (Week 2) to all three papers. Centaur predicts the average — but human behavior is heterogeneous. Willis finds different LLMs produce different strategic ecosystems — is this like cross-cultural variation? Kozlowski asks when LLM simulations are valid — maybe validity depends on which type of human the LLM is simulating. The common thread: wherever you find an average, ask what the distribution looks like underneath.
